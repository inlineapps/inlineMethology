Stripe — product shaping, written deep-dives, multi-decade abstractions 2021

Stripe deliberately does not publish a quantitative prioritisation framework. The discipline is qualitative: shape the problem before scoring it, argue in long-form writing, and filter every bet through a multi-decade direction.

Canonical secondary source: Ken Norton's "Building Products at Stripe" essay (May 18, 2021), based on his time working at the company. Important to study precisely because it's the honest "no scoring formula" exemplar.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
Most companies decide what to build by giving each idea a number (a score) and ranking. Stripe doesn't. Instead, they make people write a long document arguing why an idea matters — covering the problem, the customers it affects, what could go wrong, and what's been tried. If the writing isn't strong, the idea doesn't get built. They also ask: "would this still matter if our company is around in 30 years?" — and use that to throw away short-term-only ideas. The hard truth: this only works if your company has people who can read and judge those long documents well. Most teams can't, so they should probably score things instead. But for Stripe, careful writing + a long-term filter has produced a remarkably right rate of decisions.

The honest headline: rigour by writing, not by formula

Most frameworks on this site reduce judgement to a number (RICE), a metric (North Star), or a test result (ExP). Stripe deliberately doesn't. The rigour comes from forcing the argument onto paper — long-form memos, deep-dive docs, a written multi-decade direction — and from a culture where quality of the written case is what gets a bet funded.

This is the only entry on the impact-methods list that does not produce an expected-impact number per feature. Including it is the honest counter-example: very successful companies sometimes prioritise without scoring, by building a writing culture instead. It is genuinely hard to copy and we should be honest about that.

The three practices

No publicly-named "framework." Three observed practices, repeatedly described by Stripe's own materials and by ex-Stripe PMs (Ken Norton's essay is the best secondary source).

1 · Shaping

Shape the problem before scoring it

Norton's verbatim definition: "Shaping is the process of creating a rough solution to a concrete user problem — it fills the space between the broad strategy and the detailed product specification." PMs work the problem definition until "what to build" becomes obvious. Most teams skip this and jump to features.

2 · Deep-dives

Written, long-form, footnoted memos

Norton verbatim: "Stripe runs on written long-form documents in a way that I haven't seen before. So that means somebody can go deep, like all the way down, and then distill it back out to everybody else." Patrick Collison's emails reportedly carry footnotes. A weak proposal is exposed by the prose; no slide can hide.

3 · Multi-decade filter

"Multi-decade abstractions" as the screen

Norton verbatim: "We talk a lot about building multi-decade abstractions. I personally like to think 10 to 30 years to get out of the three- to five-year mode, but generally here people do say 'multi-decade' a lot." A direction screen, not a quarter-by-quarter score. The famous early focus on payments was this filter in action.

Sources: Ken Norton — "Building Products at Stripe" (May 18, 2021) · Stripe Atlas — "Building a great PM org" · UC Berkeley Haas Culture Kit Podcast — Patrick Collison on detail culture.
"The Collison brothers insisted on long-form memos and carefully argued proposals. The first emails I saw from our CEO literally had footnotes." — ex-Stripe staffer, summarised in published Stripe culture analyses

How Stripe actually picks the work — five steps inferred from the published practices

Reconstructed from the sources above. The mechanism is judgement-driven and writing-mediated. There is no public step that says "compute a number."

1

Identify a "concrete user problem" — a real customer's bad day

Per Norton's shaping definition, you start from a concrete user problem: a specific, narrow pain felt by a real customer (e.g. "merchants in Brazil can't accept Pix"). Not a feature, not an opportunity — a problem in customer language. ("Users having a bad day" is a common shaping-culture shorthand; Norton's own framing in the essay is "concrete user problem.")

2

Write the deep-dive — long-form, edge-cases included

What is the problem, why is it hard, what's been tried, what could we ship, what would change for the user, what's the second-order risk? Until the writer themselves believes the argument, it doesn't go forward.

3

Filter it through the multi-decade direction

"Does this still make sense in 10–30 years?" If a feature only matters in this quarter, it loses to one that compounds. This is why Stripe spent five years going deep on payments before launching adjacent products.

4

Decision is made on the written case + judgement of senior leaders

No score, no ranking spreadsheet (publicly). The bet is taken by whoever owns the decision, on the strength of the doc and the long-term filter.

5

Build it deeply — quality of detail is the success criterion

Collison's "culture that prizes details": after shipping, the success bar isn't did the metric move — it's was the artifact good enough to compound the brand. This is the part that genuinely doesn't translate to "measure-don't-estimate."

Why this is hard to copy (and easy to fake)

It requires a writing culture that already exists. "Write a deep-dive" only works if peer reviewers read it carefully, push back substantively, and treat poor prose as a red flag. Without that, deep-dives become bureaucracy — long documents that nobody reads.

It requires senior judgement absorbing the cost of mistakes. No quantitative method means the price of a wrong call is a wrong product. Stripe can absorb that because the leadership team is trusted to be right often enough. A team without that earned trust will struggle.

"Long-term filter" is easy to invoke and hard to operate. Saying "we filter for the long term" doesn't change anything. Stripe's filter actually killed short-term opportunities — that's the test of whether your version is real.

Apply to a feature sheet

Stripe's discipline doesn't produce a score — it produces a readiness checklist. If you adopt it, every backlog row tracks whether the concrete user problem is named, the deep-dive is written, the multi-decade filter passes, and a senior decision-maker has been assigned. No build starts until every cell is green and the doc has been read.

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Pick the highest-priority item on your roadmap. Open a doc. Write the concrete user problem in one sentence — naming a real customer (not a persona) and a specific bad moment. Then write three honest edge cases your team would rather not talk about. If you can't fill in either, you're at the shaping stage Stripe is good at and most teams skip. You don't need to adopt the whole culture; the shaping question alone ("whose concrete problem am I actually solving?") is the cheap part of Stripe's discipline.
Quick glossary for the columns below. Bad-day moment = a named real customer in a specific painful situation; the shorthand for Norton's "concrete user problem." Deep-dive = a long-form written memo (not slides) that engages with edge cases and history. 30-year filter = Norton's "multi-decade abstractions" framing — does this still matter if the company is around in 30 years? Defer = a verdict that's neither yes nor no; "not now, doc isn't ready or direction is unclear."

The most important reading skill on this page. Look at the Crypto on-ramp row. The deep-dive is approved (9 edge cases — high quality). The bad-day moment is real (customers are asking). But the verdict is Defer, not Build. Why? The 30-year filter failed — volatility and regulatory flux make this look short-term-only, even though it would probably win on a RICE score today. This is the gate that distinguishes Stripe's discipline from "build what customers ask for." A team without an explicit multi-decade filter would ship Crypto on-ramp and discover the regulatory cost over the following years. The filter only does work when it actually kills tempting things — otherwise it's just a phrase.
Decision rule. Four gates, all must pass. (1) Real bad-day moment named in customer terms (not "modernise" or "improve")? (2) Deep-dive written, reviewed, edge cases engaged? (3) 30-year filter passes — short-term-only wins fail this even if they're tempting. (4) Single named decision-maker assigned? Build when all pass. Re-draft when the doc is thin or the bad-day is vague — go back to writing. Defer when the 30-yr filter fails or the doc isn't ready — neither a yes nor a no, just "not now." Kill when there's no bad-day moment after iteration; that means the feature was a wishlist item, not a customer problem. This method does not score; it filters by argument quality. The price of no formula is heavier reliance on the decision-maker's judgement — that trade-off is the discipline.
Why it belongs on the list anyway. Stripe is a $90B+ private company whose product decisions have been right at a remarkable rate. If a team concludes "we'll just score things with RICE and ship," Stripe is the counter-example: great product judgement can come from disciplined writing and a long-term direction, no scoring formula required. The discipline transfers; the absence of a scoring formula is the trade-off accepted.
