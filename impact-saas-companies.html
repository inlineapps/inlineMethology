How Top SaaS / B2B Companies Decide What to Build

Ten well-documented software companies — their product-decision framework, and (where they publish one) how they estimate a feature's expected impact. Every claim is sourced; entries without a real quantitative method say so plainly.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
Key finding Scoring & Sizing Input Modeling Experimentation Goal-Laddering Written Discipline All methods compared →

The pattern across all ten

SaaS/B2B companies split into three honest camps. A few publish a real quantitative scoring or input-modeling method (Intercom, Atlassian, GitLab, Amplitude, Slack). A few are experimentation-led — they measure impact rather than forecast it (Microsoft, LinkedIn). The rest are goal-alignment frameworks that ladder work to outcomes but do not score features (Salesforce, Asana, Stripe). Knowing which is which stops us from copying a goal framework and pretending it's an impact estimator.

Scoring & Sizing

Turn each feature into a comparable number before committing.

01Intercom

Scoring
Framework: RICE scoring (its origin)  ·  Deep-dive →
  • Built by Intercom's growth team to compare project ideas against a single goal, minimizing personal bias.
  • Four factors combined into one comparable score; designed to maximize "total impact per time worked."
How they estimate impact Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort. Reach = people affected per period; Impact on a fixed scale (3 / 2 / 1 / .5 / .25); Confidence (100 / 80 / 50%) discounts un-evidenced guesses; Effort in person-months. The Impact × Confidence terms are the expected-impact estimate.
SourceIntercom — RICE

02Atlassian

Scoring
Framework: Weighted Scoring Model (+ prioritization playbook)  ·  See in comparison →
  • Publishes a guide comparing six prioritization frameworks for choosing what to build next.
  • Frames prioritization as weighing opportunities against business goals, customer value, and resources — strategy-led, not preference-led.
How they estimate impact Weighted Scoring: distribute 100 points across weighted criteria, score each feature per criterion, multiply by weight and sum. The weighted total is the expected-value proxy used to rank.
SourceAtlassian — Prioritization frameworksJira Product Discovery handbook

03GitLab

Scoring
Framework: RICE + Customer Issues Prioritization (fully public handbook)  ·  RICE deep-dive →
  • Entire product process is open in the GitLab Handbook; uses RICE-style and async-RICE prioritization across product groups.
  • A dedicated Customer Issues framework gives teams "a shared language to communicate customer impact."
How they estimate impact RICE for ranking; the customer-issues framework scores issues by whether they block sales, risk renewals, or are committed deliverables — translating qualitative feedback into impact-weighted signals.
Caveat: exact point scale comes from handbook snippets — confirm on the page before quoting precise sub-values.
SourceGitLab Handbook — Customer Issues frameworkProduct processes

Input Modeling

Tie each feature to the metric input it moves, not to raw output.  → Deep-dive: how the North Star Framework actually sets priority

04Amplitude

Input modeling
Framework: North Star Framework / Playbook  ·  Deep-dive →
  • One North Star Metric (NSM) capturing customer value, supported by 3–5 Inputs teams can directly move.
  • A "North Star tree" connects initiatives to inputs so teams see how work ladders to the NSM and can trade off.
How they estimate impact Features are judged by which Input they move and how that Input drives the NSM — not by output count. Amplitude's own NSM is Weekly Learning Users (active users who shared a learning consumed by others within 7 days).
Caveat: precise WLU input names came via search snippet — confirm on the Playbook page.
SourceAmplitude — North Star PlaybookNSM & inputs

05Slack

Input modeling
Framework: North Star + activation-input metric  ·  Deep-dive →
  • North Star is Weekly Active Teams (team-level value, not raw user count); team-level inputs like messages-sent and activation rate feed it.
  • Famous activation input: 2,000 messages sent by a team. Founder Stewart Butterfield: "after 2,000 messages, 93% of those customers are still using Slack today" — the threshold at which a team has truly tried the product.
How they estimate impact Work is judged by whether it moves the activation input that predicts retention — i.e. gets more teams to the 2,000-message threshold faster — rather than by feature count. Amplitude's own North Star Playbook uses Slack as a worked example of this input-driven model.
Caveat: the 2,000-message figure and NSM are founder/secondary accounts (First Round, Amplitude), not a published internal scoring sheet; it's an input model, not a per-feature score.
SourceFirst Round Review — Butterfield interviewAmplitude — North Star (Slack example)

Experimentation (measure, don't guess)

Define a success metric up front, ship behind a test, let data decide.  

06Microsoft

Experimentation
Framework: ExP platform · OEC · CUPED (Ron Kohavi)  ·  Deep-dive →
  • Pioneered trustworthy controlled experiments at scale; features ship on test results, not opinion.
  • Co-authors of the standard text Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments.
How they estimate impact The OEC (Overall Evaluation Criterion) is a single agreed success metric per experiment. CUPED uses pre-experiment data to cut variance, getting the same power from fewer users.

Experimentation

Goal-Laddering (alignment, not feature scoring)

These connect work to outcomes — but do not estimate per-feature impact. Use them for alignment, not as a scoring model.

08Salesforce

Goal-laddering
Framework: V2MOM — Vision, Values, Methods, Obstacles, Measures  ·  Deep-dive →
  • Strategic alignment framework created by Marc Benioff; cascades company direction so individual work ladders to top goals.
  • Only quantitative element is "Measures" (the numeric results targeted).
How they estimate impact It doesn't — V2MOM is goal-setting/alignment, not a feature-impact scoring method. Present it as disciplined goal-setting, not opportunity sizing.
SourceSalesforce — Create alignment with a V2MOM

09Asana

Goal-laddering
Framework: Pyramid of Clarity (Mission → Strategy → Objectives → Key Results → Projects)  ·  Deep-dive →
  • Every person can trace how daily work ladders to objectives and the company mission.
  • Goal: prioritize "work that drives measurable impact" via clear alignment.
How they estimate impact Goal-laddering via OKRs — impact is judged by whether a project moves a Key Result. An alignment + measurement structure, not a scoring formula.
SourceAsana — Pyramid of ClarityWavelength

Written Discipline

No scoring formula — rigor comes from forcing the argument onto paper.

10Stripe

Written discipline
Practice: Product shaping + written deep-dives + long-term-direction filter  ·  Deep-dive →
  • Disciplined product judgment over scoring: shape the problem, write deep-dive docs, filter against a multi-decade direction.
  • Uses qualitative log-lines like "users having a bad day" rather than a numeric prioritization model.
How they estimate impact No public quantitative method — impact is argued qualitatively in writing and filtered by long-term strategy. Included as the honest "great discipline, no scoring formula" exemplar.
SourceStripe Atlas — Building a great PM orgKen Norton — Building products at Stripe
