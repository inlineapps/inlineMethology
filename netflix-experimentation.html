Netflix — ABlaze, sequential testing, and causal inference for the unexperimentable

Netflix's published method: almost every product decision is an experiment, run on a platform called ABlaze. Their distinct contribution to the field — sequential testing (read results continuously without inflating false positives) and causal inference for decisions where A/B is infeasible (content investments, pricing).

The honest headline: experimentation is the default, even where it's hard

Netflix is famous for testing every UI change — but the more interesting claim is what they do when an A/B test is infeasible. You can't randomly assign half your subscribers to "Netflix without Squid Game" to measure its lift. Their answer: quasi-experiments and causal machine learning — interrupted time series, inverse-probability weighting, synthetic controls. The discipline ("nothing ships without a measured causal effect") survives even where the textbook A/B doesn't.

The reasoning rule is borrowed from Microsoft ExP: declare the success metric, measure causally, ship/kill on the result. Netflix's contributions are the methods that make that rule applicable to more decisions.

Three things Netflix does that aren't standard A/B

Mostly the same core as everyone else's platform — but three extensions worth knowing because they push the boundary of "what can be measured."

ABlaze

The platform

Internal experimentation platform — Netflix's name for the equivalent of Microsoft's ExP or LinkedIn's T-REX. Provides randomization, metric pipelines, dashboards.

Sequential

Sequential A/B testing

Lets you peek at results during the experiment and stop early on clear winners/losers — without inflating false-positive rate. The win: faster ship-decisions; the cost: more complex statistics.

Causal

Causal inference for non-A/B-able decisions

For things you can't randomize (content slate, pricing changes, regional rollouts) Netflix uses interrupted time-series, IPW, double-ML. Result: a defensible causal estimate even without a clean experiment.

Source: Netflix Research — Experimentation & Causal Inference · TechBlog — Sequential A/B testing (Part 1).

How Netflix actually picks the work

Same OEC-driven rule as Microsoft, applied through their own toolset. The interesting part is what happens before the test — they invest heavily in metric design and causal-design choices.

1

Decide whether this is A/B-able

Most product changes (UI, recommendations, playback) are. Content investments and pricing typically aren't. The decision sets the methodology.

2

Pick the OEC and guardrails

Netflix's headline metric is retention — but it's noisy and slow. Most experiments use proxy metrics with established correlation to retention (engagement, completion, session length) plus retention as the long-run check.

3

Choose the design — A/B, sequential, or quasi-experimental

A/B for randomizable changes. Sequential when speed matters. Quasi-experiment when randomization isn't possible.

4

Run, monitor, decide

The platform reads out the OEC. Sequential designs may stop early; classical designs run to pre-declared sample size.

5

Re-evaluate the metric model itself

Netflix periodically re-tests which proxy metrics actually correlate with long-run retention — keeping the OEC honest over time. Most companies skip this; it's why their experiments stay trustworthy at scale.

The transferable insight: experimentation isn't just A/B. Most teams equate "experimentation" with "split-test the button colour." Netflix's contribution is showing that the discipline (causal estimate of expected impact) is portable to decisions that can't be randomized — by using time-series, regional, and quasi-experimental designs. That means even "non-A/B-able" items (a partnership integration, a one-off campaign, a pricing change) can still be evaluated causally with the right design.
