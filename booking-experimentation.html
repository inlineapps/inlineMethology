Booking.com — democratised experimentation as company culture

Booking's distinctive contribution isn't a clever technique — it's a culture: any employee can launch an experiment on millions of users without management sign-off. The platform's job is to enforce trust (guardrails, SRM, defined metrics), not to gatekeep ideas.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.

The honest headline: trust the platform, not the manager

Most experimentation cultures gate ideas at a person: a director, a council, a roadmap review. Booking gates at the platform: if your hypothesis passes the automated guardrails, you can ship the test — no one's permission required. That single inversion is what enables ~25,000 tests/year at the company level.

The famous Stefan Thomke HBR write-up calls it "Building a Culture of Experimentation" — and the load-bearing word is culture. The platform is the enforcement layer; the culture is the choice to let employees act on what the platform clears.

Three pillars that make the culture safe

Anyone can launch a test, but the platform won't let an unsafe one through. The pillars are the auto-enforced trust checks.

Hypothesis

Written before the test runs

Every experiment must declare its hypothesis, OEC, and guardrails in a templated form. The system rejects tests without them. Forces "what would change my mind?" up front.

SRM checks

Sample-Ratio-Mismatch — auto-flagged

If treatment / control groups end up unequal in size (a sign of bad randomization or instrumentation), the platform refuses to read out the result. This single check kills a huge fraction of false wins industry-wide.

Standard metrics

Defined once, used everywhere

The OEC, guardrails, and supporting metrics are shared definitions across teams. Removes the "my metric vs your metric" argument; makes cross-experiment comparison honest.

Source: HBR — Building a culture of experimentation (Thomke, March 2020) · Paper — Democratizing Online Controlled Experiments at Booking.com.

How Booking actually picks what ships

There is no "prioritization meeting" in the classical sense. The mechanism is cheap-to-launch + guardrails-enforced = let the test decide.

1

Anyone writes the hypothesis

A designer, an engineer, a PM. Templated: "If we change X, OEC will move by Y because Z." The template is the gate.

2

Platform validates and provisions

Auto-checks the hypothesis is complete; assigns randomization; sets the OEC and guardrails; opens the experiment.

3

Run, with SRM and guardrails watching

If anything goes off the rails (SRM, guardrail breach), the test is paused. No human in the loop required.

4

Read the OEC. Ship, kill, or iterate.

The platform reports significance against the pre-declared OEC. The author decides what to do — no management sign-off step.

5

Cumulative culture: ~25,000 tests/year

Because the cost-to-launch is near-zero, the rate of experiments per employee per year is the highest publicly reported. That volume is itself the prioritization mechanism — bad ideas die fast, good ones reveal themselves.

The "sold-out properties" example — why you can't guess

A finding no PM would have proposed

One of Booking's most-quoted experiments: showing sold-out properties alongside available ones increased bookings. The signal was social-proof / scarcity — these places are good, others have already booked them — pushing diners to book the available alternatives faster.

No prioritization framework would have ranked "show users things they can't buy" highly. The experiment found it. That's the entire case for the culture: the wins you'd never have predicted are why measurement beats estimation.

Source: Thomke, HBR 2020.

The transferable insight: cheap-to-launch is the lever. The reason Booking's culture works is that the cost to run a test is so low that not running one is the unusual choice. For a team our size, "cheap" means automated guardrails and a single shared metric definition — not 100 data scientists. The expensive part is the discipline of writing the hypothesis and OEC every time.
