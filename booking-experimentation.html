Booking.com — democratised experimentation as company culture 2017

Booking's distinctive contribution isn't a clever technique — it's a culture: any employee can launch an experiment on millions of users without management sign-off.

The platform's job is to enforce trust (guardrails, SRM, defined metrics), not to gatekeep ideas. Canonical sources: Vermeer et al. "Democratizing Online Controlled Experiments at Booking.com" (2017 arxiv preprint) and Stefan Thomke's HBR write-up (March–April 2020).

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
Most companies have a manager decide which feature ideas get tested. Booking flipped this: anyone — designer, engineer, intern — can launch a real A/B test on millions of users, without asking permission. The trick that makes this safe: the platform won't let your test run unless you've written down what would count as a win before it starts, and the platform automatically catches when something's broken. So nobody can cheat, and bad ideas die fast on their own. The result: 25,000 experiments every year. That's how they discovered weird wins no manager would have approved — like showing people hotels they couldn't book, which somehow made more people book others.

The honest headline: trust the platform, not the manager

Most experimentation cultures gate ideas at a person: a director, a council, a roadmap review. Booking gates at the platform: if your hypothesis passes the automated guardrails, you can ship the test — no one's permission required. That single inversion is what enables ~25,000 tests/year at the company level.

The work is most associated with Lukas Vermeer (Booking's senior product owner who built much of the culture) and Stefan Thomke's HBR write-up "Building a Culture of Experimentation". The platform is the enforcement layer; the culture is the choice to let employees act on what the platform clears.

Three pillars that make the culture safe

Anyone can launch a test, but the platform won't let an unsafe one through. The pillars are the auto-enforced trust checks.

Hypothesis

Written before the test runs

Every experiment must declare its hypothesis, OEC, and guardrails in a templated form. The system rejects tests without them. Forces "what would change my mind?" up front.

SRM checks

Sample-Ratio-Mismatch — auto-flagged

If treatment / control groups end up unequal in size (a sign of bad randomization or instrumentation), the platform refuses to read out the result. This single check kills a huge fraction of false wins industry-wide.

Standard metrics

Defined once, used everywhere

The OEC, guardrails, and supporting metrics are shared definitions across teams. Removes the "my metric vs your metric" argument; makes cross-experiment comparison honest.

Sources: Vermeer et al. — "Democratizing Online Controlled Experiments at Booking.com" (arxiv preprint, October 2017) · Stefan Thomke — "Building a Culture of Experimentation" (HBR, March–April 2020 issue).

How Booking actually picks what ships

There is no "prioritization meeting" in the classical sense. The mechanism is cheap-to-launch + guardrails-enforced = let the test decide.

1

Anyone writes the hypothesis

A designer, an engineer, a PM. Templated: "If we change X, OEC will move by Y because Z." The template is the gate.

2

Platform validates and provisions

Auto-checks the hypothesis is complete; assigns randomization; sets the OEC and guardrails; opens the experiment.

3

Run, with SRM and guardrails watching

If anything goes off the rails (SRM, guardrail breach), the test is paused. No human in the loop required.

4

Read the OEC. Ship, kill, or iterate.

The platform reports significance against the pre-declared OEC. The author decides what to do — no management sign-off step.

5

Cumulative culture: ~25,000 tests/year

Because the cost-to-launch is near-zero, the rate of experiments per employee per year is the highest publicly reported. That volume is itself the prioritization mechanism — bad ideas die fast, good ones reveal themselves.

The "sold-out properties" example — why you can't guess

A finding no PM would have proposed

One of Booking's most-quoted experiments: showing sold-out properties alongside available ones increased bookings. The signal was social-proof / scarcity — these places are good, others have already booked them — pushing travellers to book the available alternatives faster.

No prioritization framework would have ranked "show users things they can't buy" highly. The experiment found it. That's the entire case for the culture: the wins you'd never have predicted are why measurement beats estimation.

Source: Thomke, HBR 2020.

Apply to a feature sheet

Booking's discipline turns the backlog into an always-on experiment queue. With 1,000+ concurrent tests, the sheet doesn't have a "this quarter's bets" view — it has what's running right now. Columns mirror Microsoft ExP with Booking-specific OEC (conversion to booking, revenue per visitor) and guardrails (cancellation rate, refund rate).

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Walk through your team's typical "we want to try X" path. Count the management approvals between idea and live test. Each approval is a tax on your experiment rate. Pick the lowest-stakes one and ask out loud: "what does this approval actually catch that a guardrail wouldn't?" If the honest answer is "nothing — it's a checkbox," you've identified a step to replace with an automated check. Booking's leverage isn't a smarter platform; it's having converted human checks into platform checks one at a time, over years.
Quick glossary. OEC = Overall Evaluation Criterion (the agreed success metric). SRM = Sample-Ratio Mismatch (auto-flags broken randomization). Guardrails = metrics that must not regress. Cancellation rate & refund rate = Booking-specific marketplace guardrails — a feature that boosts bookings by tricking users into commitments they later cancel is a net loss; these two metrics make that visible.

The most important reading skill on this page. The sold-out row is the table's most important entry — but not for the +1.3% conversion lift. It matters because no human prioritization framework would have ranked it as a candidate worth shipping. "Show users things they can't buy" sounds like a UX mistake. The only way the experiment got to run is that Booking's culture lets anyone launch a test without a manager's prior judgment about whether the idea sounds smart. That's the load-bearing claim of the entire page: the wins you wouldn't have predicted are the ones democratized testing is for. If your culture requires a manager to approve every test, you systematically filter out the surprising wins.
Decision rule. Standard OEC + guardrails — but Booking's two distinguishing guardrails are cancellation rate and refund rate. The cross-sell row shows why: a feature that lifts revenue by tricking users into bookings they cancel is worse than no feature. The sold-out row is the published canonical: nobody would have guessed that showing things users can't buy would increase bookings — only an experiment found it. That's the entire case for measure-don't-estimate at this scale.
The transferable insight: cheap-to-launch is the lever. The reason Booking's culture works is that the cost to run a test is so low that not running one is the unusual choice. For a team our size, "cheap" means automated guardrails and a single shared metric definition — not 100 data scientists. The expensive part is the discipline of writing the hypothesis and OEC every time.
