Spotify DIBB + Bets Board — chain a bet to evidence

DIBB = Data → Insight → Belief → Bet. A four-step chain that says: before committing to a bet, prove there's data behind the insight, the insight is real, and the belief follows from it. Paired with a "Bets Board" that limits how many big bets the company runs concurrently.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.

The honest headline: a strategic narrative test, not a scoring formula

DIBB doesn't tell you whether feature A beats feature B by a number. It asks four questions and refuses to fund any bet that can't answer them: What's the data? What insight do we draw? What do we now believe? What bet does that imply? A bet that skips a layer — most commonly, jumping from belief to bet without insight — is not approved.

It's a narrative consistency check. Combined with the Bets Board (limit to a few large bets at once), it forces focus: you can't run ten bets if each requires a written DIBB chain.

DIBB — the four-link chain

The discipline is the arrow: each step must justify the next. A weak link breaks the whole chain.

D
Data
Observed numbers we can point to. Not anecdotes, not gut.
I
Insight
What the data means. A pattern, a gap, a surprise.
B
Belief
What we now think will work — derived from the insight.
B
Bet
The committed action — testable, sized, time-bound.

The Bets Board — a Kanban for big company bets

Each bet is a card. The board limits work-in-progress — a bet can't move to "Now" until something leaves it. Each card carries a 2-page DIBB doc + sponsor + success metrics.

How DIBB actually picks the bets

Five steps, but the test happens between the steps — each arrow has to hold up under questioning. Most bets die between Insight and Belief.

1

Start from observed Data

Hard numbers from product analytics, customer research, or market data. Not "we noticed" — a specific data point with a source.

2

Extract an Insight

What does the data mean? A surprise, a gap, a behavioural pattern. If multiple plausible insights, name them — the choice is part of the argument.

3

State the Belief explicitly

"We now believe …" — a single-sentence claim about cause-and-effect or user behaviour. The Belief must follow from the Insight; if it doesn't, you're guessing.

4

Make the Bet — sized, sponsored, success metrics declared

The Bet inherits the Belief. A 2-page doc names: sponsor, stakeholders, expected outcome, success metric, deadline. Cards go onto the Bets Board, one per active bet.

5

Validate the bet — track the success metric, re-evaluate quarterly

Bets aren't fire-and-forget. The board is reviewed; cards move (Won, Lost, Won't Do) so the org learns which Beliefs were right.

Honest sourcing caveat

There is no first-party Spotify post that defines DIBB. The best sources are Henrik Kniberg (ex-Spotify agile coach, well-positioned but a consultant, not a Spotify-PR statement) and secondary PM sites. Treat DIBB as a second-party account — it's well-evidenced as a real Spotify practice, but the framework name and exact mechanics live in community writing, not in Spotify's own documentation.

The Spotify "squads, tribes, guilds" model has the same sourcing pattern — Kniberg's whitepapers were the public artifact. The shape is real; the formality may differ inside Spotify.
Why DIBB is on the list anyway. The narrative chain — every bet must trace back through Belief → Insight → Data — is the single best antidote to the common planning failure mode of "we just feel like this is right." Even if the framework name varies inside Spotify, the discipline transfers cleanly.
