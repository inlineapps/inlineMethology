RICE — Intercom's transparent ranking formula 2018

A four-factor score Intercom's growth team built to rank project ideas against a single goal — designed (per the original post) to avoid bias toward features the team would use themselves. Not a forecaster; a way to make assumptions visible and rankable.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version

You've got more ideas than time. RICE turns each idea into one number so you can rank them. The number comes from four honest answers: how many people it helps, how much it helps each one, how sure you are, and how long it takes. Multiply the first three, divide by the last. Bigger number = build it first.

Everything below is how to answer those four questions without fooling yourself.

The honest headline: it's a transparent ranking, not a forecast

RICE doesn't predict revenue or guarantee impact. It takes four numbers you would have guessed anyway — Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort — and forces them onto a shared scale so two ideas are directly comparable. The single most important discipline is writing down what you actually believe, in numbers. A weak Confidence score is the formula's way of catching an overconfident estimate before it gets shipped.

The formula: RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort — designed to maximize "total impact per time worked." It is widely used by thousands of PM teams precisely because it's cheap, comparable, and falsifiable — not because it's accurate. Actual expected impact still needs evidence (past data, a fake-door test, or a controlled experiment); RICE just produces the audit trail of why we picked this over that.

Anatomy: four factors, one formula

Each factor uses a fixed scale — a pre-agreed set of values everyone scores against, not free-form numbers — so different reviewers produce comparable scores. The Confidence multiplier does the heavy lifting: it discounts ideas where you don't actually know the numbers, so confident-sounding guesses can't outrank measured evidence.

How RICE actually picks work

RICE is a four-step ritual, not a one-shot calculation. Step 5 — review the results sceptically — is what separates teams that use RICE well from teams that blindly ship the top of the list.

1

Agree the goal first

RICE compares ideas against one goal — e.g. "growth in trial signups" or "reduce support contacts." If you skip this, Impact has nothing to mean. (Intercom's example was scored against "improving conversion to paid.")

2

Score each idea on R, I, C, E

Use the fixed scales. Force Confidence honestly: an idea with no data is 50%, not 80%. That single number stops "we feel strongly" from outranking "we measured."

3

Compute and rank

Sort by the score. Ideas with similar scores are not meaningfully different — RICE is a coarse ordering, not a precise ranking.

4

Sanity-check the top of the list

Read the top 5–10 and ask "does this list make sense?" If a beloved idea ranks low, the value is in why: usually low Reach or low Confidence. Don't override the score — fix the inputs (do research to lift Confidence, or accept the verdict).

5

After shipping: log estimate vs actual

The under-used step. The Intercom post tells teams to sort and re-evaluate after the initial scoring; the same discipline applies once features ship — track which Impact estimates were wildly wrong, and over time your scale calibrates. RICE only gets better than gut-feel if you do this part.

Apply to a feature sheet

If you adopt RICE for your backlog, every feature row gains six columns. The first four are honest inputs, the fifth is computed, the sixth is the verdict. The worked example above is what the sheet looks like once it's filled in.

Where RICE breaks (and what to do instead)

The most common abuse: treating the score as truth instead of an exposed argument. The Intercom post itself flags these.

Three honest limits

  • Spurious precision. A score of 2,667 vs 2,540 is meaningless — RICE separates rough tiers, not exact ranks. Treat scores as equal if they're within ~25% of each other.
  • It doesn't tell you which goal to score against. That's the North Star Framework's job. RICE is the second step, not the first. See North Star deep-dive.
  • It penalises discovery work. Genuinely novel bets have low Confidence, so they always lose to obvious incremental wins. Counter: ring-fence a "discovery budget" outside the RICE list, or run a quick fake-door test (a fake button or landing page for a feature that doesn't exist yet, used to measure real interest) to lift Confidence before re-scoring.
