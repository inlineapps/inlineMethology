RICE — Intercom's transparent ranking formula

A four-factor score Intercom's growth team built to compare project ideas against a single goal "while minimizing personal bias." Not a forecaster — a way to make assumptions visible and rankable.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.

The honest headline: it's a transparent ranking, not a forecast

RICE doesn't predict revenue. It takes four numbers you would have guessed anyway — Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort — and forces them onto a shared scale so two ideas are comparable. Most of its value is in step zero: "write down what you actually believe." A weak Confidence score is the formula's way of catching a bullshit estimate before it ships.

The formula: RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort — designed to maximize "total impact per time worked." It is widely used (Intercom, GitLab, thousands of PM teams) precisely because it's cheap, comparable, and falsifiable — not because it's accurate.

Anatomy: four factors, one formula

Each factor uses a fixed scale so different reviewers produce comparable scores. The Confidence multiplier is the secret sauce — it discounts ideas where you don't actually know the numbers.

Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort
R
Reach
How many people / events affected per time period (e.g. customers per quarter).
Scale: raw count
I
Impact
How much it moves the chosen goal per person — picked from a fixed scale, not made up.
Scale: 3 / 2 / 1 / 0.5 / 0.25
C
Confidence
How sure you are in the R and I numbers — backed by data, evidence, or just a hunch.
Scale: 100% / 80% / 50%
E
Effort
Total person-months of work across product, design, and engineering.
Scale: person-months

Scales above are Intercom's published defaults — teams may adapt, but the discipline is the same: fixed scale + Confidence multiplier so guesses can't hide as facts.

Source: Intercom — RICE: simple prioritization for product managers (Sean McBride, original 2016 post).

How RICE actually picks work

RICE is a four-step ritual, not a one-shot calculation. Step 5 — review the results sceptically — is what separates teams that use RICE well from teams that blindly ship the top of the list.

1

Agree the goal first

RICE compares ideas against one goal — e.g. "growth in trial signups" or "reduce support contacts." If you skip this, Impact has nothing to mean. (Intercom's example was scored against "improving conversion to paid.")

2

Score each idea on R, I, C, E

Use the fixed scales. Force Confidence honestly: an idea with no data is 50%, not 80%. That single number stops "we feel strongly" from outranking "we measured."

3

Compute and rank

Sort by the score. Ideas with similar scores are not meaningfully different — RICE is a coarse ordering, not a precise ranking.

4

Sanity-check the top of the list

Read the top 5–10 and ask "does this list make sense?" If a beloved idea ranks low, the value is in why: usually low Reach or low Confidence. Don't override the score — fix the inputs (do research to lift Confidence, or accept the verdict).

5

After shipping: log estimate vs actual

The under-used step. Intercom: "the score is only a guess until you ship and measure." Track which Impact estimates were wildly wrong — over time your scale calibrates.

Items ranked low because of low Confidence aren't a cut — they're candidates for discovery work (user research, fake-door test) to lift Confidence before re-scoring next cycle. Where RICE breaks (and what to do instead)

The most common abuse: treating the score as truth instead of an exposed argument. The Intercom post itself flags these.

Three honest limits

  • Spurious precision. A score of 2,667 vs 2,540 is meaningless — RICE separates "buckets," not points. Round to within ~25%.
  • It doesn't tell you which goal to score against. That's the North Star Framework's job. RICE is the second step, not the first. See North Star deep-dive.
  • It penalises discovery work. Genuinely novel bets have low Confidence, so they always lose to obvious incremental wins. Counter: ring-fence a "discovery budget" outside the RICE list, or use to lift Confidence before scoring.
RICE ≠ feature impact estimator. RICE ranks candidates against a goal you already chose. The actual expected impact still needs evidence — past data, fake-door test, or a controlled experiment. RICE is the audit trail of why we picked this over that, not the proof it will work.
