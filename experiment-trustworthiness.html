How big tech actually runs 40,000 A/B tests per day — without drowning in noise 2010

A grounded answer to three reasonable challenges to the "experimentation works" story: what does 40k/day really count? How is interference between concurrent tests handled? How long does a real decision actually take?

No magic — the answers are public, well-cited, and older than most people realise. Foundational source: Google's Overlapping Experiment Infrastructure (Tang, Agarwal, O'Brien & Meyer — KDD 2010), with later work from Microsoft (Kohavi/Tang/Xu, 2013–2020) and LinkedIn (2020+).

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
When you hear that LinkedIn or Booking "run 40,000 experiments a day," three sensible doubts pop up. (1) That can't be 40,000 totally separate tests, right? Correct — most of them are different ramp stages or different country variants of the same idea. The honest number of distinct decisions is in the hundreds-to-thousands per day, still huge. (2) Don't all those tests mess each other up? They would, except Google solved this in 2007 (published 2010) by splitting the codebase into "layers" — different subsystems each get their own random coin-flip per user, so tests in different layers don't contaminate each other. (3) Can you really decide overnight if a number went up? No — the field requires at least 7 days to absorb weekday-vs-weekend differences. Most tests run 1–2 weeks. The whole discipline is built to prevent the "the number's up, ship it" mistake.

The honest answer to all three concerns

Your scepticism is the right instinct — and the published mechanics happen to support it on each point:

  • 40k/day is real but it's a count of variant evaluations, not 40k independent A/B tests against the whole user base. It includes every ramp stage, every per-segment variant, and every metric computation. The underlying claim — "thousands of decisions per quarter resolved by experiment" — is real; the marketing-friendly headline number isn't a one-to-one count.
  • Interference is real and the solution is older than most blogs: Google's Overlapping Experiment Infrastructure (Tang et al., KDD 2010; deployed at Google since ~2007). Every user is independently randomised across layers. Tests in different layers can stack without contaminating each other; tests in the same layer are mutually exclusive. This is the only reason "thousands of concurrent tests" is possible.
  • Nobody decides in a morning. Microsoft (Kohavi/Tang/Xu, 2020) and the broader literature: minimum 1 full week to absorb day-of-week effects; 1–4 weeks typical; longer if novelty/primacy effects are likely. A "tomorrow morning the number went up" decision is exactly what the field's discipline is built to prevent.

1. What "40,000 experiments per day" actually counts

LinkedIn's T-REX engineering posts cite "more than 40,000 experiments per day on ~8,000 metrics." Real number, but it's a sum of several different things, not 40,000 independent A/B tests fighting for the same user base. The breakdown matters:

What goes into LinkedIn's daily count (illustrative — the literal split isn't published, the categories are)

Each ramp stage of the same feature counted separately (1% → 5% → 25% → 50% → 100% = 5 entries)many
Per-segment versions of the same change (per-country, per-device, per-locale) counted as distinct experimentsmany
Multivariate tests counted per arm (an A/B/C/D test = 4 entries)many
Concurrent independent A/B tests on different surfaces (genuine parallel decisions)many
Long-running always-on holdouts and quasi-experimentssome
Total daily experiment-evaluations rolled up across all categories40k+

So the more honest reading: LinkedIn is running hundreds-to-low-thousands of distinct product decisions simultaneously, each producing many counted evaluations as it ramps, segments, and accumulates significance. That's still extraordinary — and the layered infrastructure below is why it works without chaos. It's not 40k unrelated tests treating users like a casino chip table.

Source: LinkedIn — T-REX team page (40k/day, 8k metrics) · XLNT platform paper. The breakdown above is inferred from how published experimentation platforms compose their counts — LinkedIn doesn't publish the literal split.

2. The interference problem — and the layered-randomization trick that solves it

Your concern in your words: "if test A and test B both affect metric A, you can't say A caused the lift." Exactly right. This is a real, well-documented problem. The published solution is one of the most important ideas in modern experimentation: orthogonal layered randomization.

"The infrastructure had been used in Google since around 2007 … key concepts include domains (a segmentation of traffic), layers (corresponding to a subset of the system parameters), and experiments (a segmentation of traffic where zero or more system parameters can be given alternate values)." — Tang, Agarwal, O'Brien & Meyer, Overlapping Experiment Infrastructure: More, Better, Faster Experimentation, KDD 2010 (pp. 17–26).

The trick in one paragraph

Treat the codebase as a stack of independent subsystems (search-ranking, notifications, ads, homepage hero, onboarding, …). Each subsystem is a layer. Within a layer, only one experiment can touch a user at a time (mutually exclusive — true A/B integrity for that subsystem). Across layers, the user is randomised independently for each layer using a different hash, so the assignment in layer L1 carries no statistical relationship with the assignment in L2. Because the layers were defined to not share parameters, the tests can't contaminate each other.

Each user's layer assignments are statistically independent — they're hashed with a per-layer salt, so being treatment in Layer 1 says nothing about being treatment in Layer 2. Test A's treatment vs control comparison is clean because across users, the other layers' assignments are balanced 50/50 in both A's treatment and A's control groups. Same for Test B and Test C. This is why three tests can run on the same user without spoiling each other's reads.

"But what if two tests do touch the same metric?"

Then they cannot live in different layers — the layer boundary was wrong. Three real mitigations from published platforms:

  • Put them in the same layer — mutually exclusive. One at a time. Cost: queueing; benefit: zero contamination. (LaunchDarkly, Optimizely, AB Tasty all implement this as "mutually exclusive experiments" — standard third-party feature.)
  • Run a full factorial — A/B × C/D = four arms, measure interaction explicitly. Kohavi/Henne/Sommerfield (KDD 2007) put the equivalent point as: "Strong interactions are rare in practice; awareness is usually enough. Pairwise statistical tests can auto-flag" — i.e. if you suspect interaction, run multivariate, don't run parallel.
  • Ghosting (Booking.com's published practice) — log the counterfactual model score / UI state the user would have seen alongside the served version, so you can study interactions before committing to a live factorial.
The empirical-frequency answer to "but interactions must be everywhere": Kohavi/Henne/Sommerfield (KDD 2007, verbatim): "Strong interactions are rare in practice; awareness is usually enough." Translated: most pairs of tests at scale don't interact because most subsystems are actually independent, and the discipline of layer assignment forces engineers to declare which subsystem they're touching before launching. The framework's discipline doesn't deny interactions — it makes them visible up front so the test is either gated or designed as multivariate.
Sources: Tang et al. — Overlapping Experiment Infrastructure (PDF, KDD 2010) · Google Research listing · Booking.com layered experimentation (summary) · Kohavi/Tang/Xu, Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments (Cambridge, 2020) — Ch. 18 "Variance Estimation & Improved Sensitivity" + Ch. 22 "Learnings from Running A/B Tests."

3. Duration — why no one decides "the next morning"

Your point: an overnight read isn't a decision; you need a real measurement window to know whether the move is signal or noise. The literature agrees, with specific minimums.

Published practice on test duration

Hard minimum — full 7 days so day-of-week effects are absorbed (weekend behaviour ≠ weekday)7d
Typical at Microsoft / Bing — most experiments run 1–2 weeks (Kohavi/Tang/Xu, 2020)7–14d
If novelty effect suspected — initial 1–2 week spike fades, so read at week 2–3 not week 114–21d
If primacy effect suspected — habituated users need weeks to adapt; long-term holdout (Uber) or 4+ weeks28d+
Power-determined — duration must hit a pre-computed sample-size threshold; underpowered tests are not "shorter" — they're inconclusiveN-driven

"The number is up after one day" is exactly the trap the field's training material is built to prevent. Recent academic work (Larsen et al., Setting the duration of online A/B experiments, arXiv 2024) shows the duration choice itself is a decision under uncertainty — pre-declared, not adjusted mid-flight.

Sources: Kohavi, Tang & Xu, Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments (Cambridge, 2020); Larsen et al., "Setting the duration of online A/B experiments" (arXiv:2408.02830, 2024); Kohavi et al., "Practical Guide to Controlled Experiments on the Web" (2007).

4. Variance, standard deviation & "is it actually different?"

"Up by 2%" might be inside the daily noise. The discipline is to ask: is the observed lift larger than the metric's known variance at this sample size?

For each metric the team computes a baseline standard deviation from pre-experiment data. The required sample size to detect a desired minimum effect (MDE) is roughly:

N ≈ 16 × (σ ÷ MDE)2 (rough rule-of-thumb for two-sample 80% power, α=0.05)

So smaller effects need quadratically more users — the reason "we'll just test it small" doesn't fly for subtle changes. The discipline of pre-declaring the MDE, the OEC, and the duration before launching is what stops the "the number's up, let's ship" failure mode.

Three published tricks that don't change duration much but make reads more honest

  • CUPED (Microsoft, 2013) — use each user's pre-experiment behaviour as a covariate; shrinks confidence interval ~50% → same answer from ~half the sample size.
  • Sequential testing (Netflix, Optimizely, others) — peek continuously but with adjusted significance thresholds (mSPRT, group-sequential designs) so the false-positive rate stays controlled. Lets you stop early on clear winners or losers without the classic "p-hacking by repeated peeking" problem.
  • SRM check (Sample Ratio Mismatch) — if treatment / control end up 51% / 49% instead of 50/50, the platform refuses to read out. A skewed split means the randomization was broken. (The "~70% of surprising results turn out to be bugs" figure widely repeated in this context is from Kohavi's talks / Trustworthy book commentary, not from a single published paper — treat as cultural finding.)

5. A concrete worked example — what a real 2-week experiment looks like

Illustrative reconstruction of how a typical staged-rollout experiment plays out — built from Microsoft / Booking / Uber published patterns. Numbers are realistic but illustrative.

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Pick the most recent experiment your team called a "win." Audit it against the three skeptic questions on this page: (1) Did the ramp run for at least 7 days at the read window (not "we saw it go up on day 2")? (2) Was there any concurrent test in the same layer / subsystem that could have contaminated the result? (3) Did the confidence interval on the OEC actually exclude zero, or did the team eyeball the headline number? If the answer to any is "no," the win is less trustworthy than it looks — and that's a finding worth surfacing before the next decision rests on it.

Because the 95% CI on the OEC does not include 0 (the lower bound is +0.6%), we say the lift is statistically significant. Guardrails held. SRM clean. → Decision: ship to 100%. If the OEC's CI had been [−0.2%, +1.4%], the lift would be inconclusive (CI includes 0) — you'd extend the test, not ship.

The most important reading skill on this page. Look at the Day-15 readout table. The OEC moved +1.8%. Most teams would call this a win and move on. But the load-bearing column is the 95% confidence interval: [+0.6%, +3.0%]. The lower bound is +0.6% — barely above zero. If we'd hit "ship" the moment the headline showed positive, we'd be acting on a number whose real effect could plausibly be as small as 0.6%. The discipline isn't reading the central estimate; it's checking whether the entire confidence interval sits where you'd ship. The CI is the honest signal; the point estimate is the marketing pitch. A team that ships off the point estimate alone is shipping noise as often as signal.
Why two weeks is the floor, not the ceiling. Day-of-week effects alone require a full 7 days. Adding a second week is the cheapest insurance against a Day-8 reversal (novelty effect tailing off, marketing campaign ending, weekend traffic mix shifting). Kohavi et al. recommend 1–2 weeks as the default at Microsoft; longer for novelty-prone surfaces.
