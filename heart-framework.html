Google HEART + Goals-Signals-Metrics — defining what success looks like

From Google Research (Rodden, Hutchinson, Fu — CHI 2010): five UX outcome categories (HEART) plus a three-step ladder (Goals → Signals → Metrics) that turns intent into measurable numbers. Used to set the success bar before an experiment runs.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.

The honest headline: a metric-picker, not an impact estimator

HEART/GSM is a tool for answering "what does success look like for this feature?" before you ship. It picks the metrics. Whether the feature actually moves those metrics is decided later — usually by a controlled experiment.

So Google's published method is split: HEART/GSM at the front ("here's the metric we expect to move") + experimentation at the back ("here's the lift we actually measured"). Either half alone is incomplete.

The anatomy — HEART is five dimensions, GSM is the ladder

HEART says "here are five things a UX could move." GSM says "for any goal you pick, ladder it down to a measurable metric in three steps." Use HEART as the menu, GSM as the cookbook.

H
Happiness
Subjective satisfaction (surveys, NPS).
E
Engagement
Frequency, depth, intensity of use.
A
Adoption
New users / new feature uptake.
R
Retention
Returning users over time.
T
Task success
Completion / time / error rates.

Goals → Signals → Metrics — the ladder

For each HEART dimension you care about, ladder it down through three layers. The discipline is that every metric must trace upward to a stated goal — no orphan metrics.

LayerQuestionExample (Engagement)
GoalWhat is the user trying to do?Find & rebook restaurants they liked.
SignalWhat observable behaviour shows progress / failure?Return visits to a merchant page; re-booking same restaurant.
MetricHow do we count that signal?% of bookings from previously-visited merchants per user / month
Source: Google Research — Rodden, Hutchinson, Fu, "Measuring the User Experience on a Large Scale" (CHI 2010) · IxDF explainer.

How HEART/GSM actually picks the metrics

Four steps. Step 1 — picking which HEART dimensions to ignore — is more important than which to include.

1

Choose 2–3 HEART dimensions, not all five

Most features don't move all five. A new search UI is about Task-success + Engagement, not Happiness. Naming the dimensions you're not measuring is the discipline that keeps the metric list short.

2

For each dimension, write a goal in user language

"User can rebook in < 3 clicks" — not "the booking funnel is faster." The goal must be observable from the user's perspective.

3

Identify signals — observable behaviours

What action would a user take that proves the goal is met? A signal is more concrete than a goal but not yet a number.

4

Pick the metric — how the signal is counted

The metric is the formula. Often there are several candidates per signal — pick the one cheapest to compute and least gameable.

Apply to a feature sheet

HEART/GSM doesn't rank features — it tells you what metric each feature owes a result on. If you adopt it, every shipped feature in your backlog gains a HEART row: which dimension(s) it moves, the goal/signal/metric trio, baseline, target, actual, and the decision you make once the data lands.

Style note borrowed from Google's CHI paper: pick 2–3 HEART dimensions per feature (not all five), and always include at least one guardrail from a dimension you're not trying to lift (Happiness is the common choice).

Decision rule. Keep when the primary metric reaches (or comes close to) target and the guardrail holds. "Niche" is a legal verdict — Confidential mode kept because it serves a small segment well without harming others. Iterate when the metric moved less than expected but guardrails hold; change the variant, re-run. Roll back when the guardrail breaches (the reading-pane row: faster but unhappier). Kill when the metric didn't move and there's no obvious next iteration. HEART/GSM defines what winning looks like; controlled experiments tell you whether you won.

What HEART/GSM does not do

It does not estimate the lift. HEART picks which metric to watch; it does not say "we expect +5pp." That number comes from prior data, a RICE Impact score, a PR-FAQ Internal FAQ, or a controlled experiment.

It will produce too many metrics if you pick all five HEART dimensions. The whole point of the framework is forcing trade-offs — discipline yourself to 2–3.
Why HEART is on the list. It is the cheapest, most teachable way to stop teams from saying "we'll just look at engagement" without naming a number. Five letters, one ladder — a 30-minute exercise per feature.
