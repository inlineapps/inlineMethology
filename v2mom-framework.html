V2MOM — Salesforce's five-line alignment cascade 1999

Marc Benioff drafted Salesforce's first V2MOM on the back of an American Express envelope during an American Airlines flight in April 1999 — the document is dated 4/12/1999. Five questions every leader, team, and individual answers in one page.

It is an alignment mechanism, not a feature-impact estimator — and being honest about that is the whole point.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
V2MOM is five questions you (and everyone in the company) answer on one page. Vision: what are we trying to do? Values: what won't we compromise on? Methods: how will we do it? Obstacles: what's in the way? Measures: how will we know it worked? The CEO writes one, then every team writes one that fits inside it, then every person writes one that fits inside their team's. If your page doesn't connect to the page above you, that's a problem worth talking about. It doesn't pick which feature to build — it picks which direction your features should pull in.

The honest headline: it's a one-page cascade, not a scoring formula

V2MOM doesn't tell you what to build. It tells you what you're trying to do, what you'll refuse to compromise on, how you'll do it, what's in the way, and how you'll know it worked — written by every leader, cascaded to every team. The discipline is that everyone in the company can read the CEO's V2MOM and write a coherent one of their own that lines up with it.

Benioff's own framing (verbatim): "Without a doubt, this process has been our best-kept secret to the fast growth and excellence we have achieved." Useful precisely because it doesn't pretend to estimate per-feature impact — it forces clarity at the level above.

The five letters

Every V2MOM answers the same five questions in the same order. The ordering is load-bearing: you can't write a sensible "Method" until you've written the "Values" that constrain it.

V
Vision
"What do you want to achieve?" — Benioff. One sentence, ambitious, unambiguous.
V
Values
"What's important to you?" — the principles that guide the work; what we will not compromise on.
M
Methods
"How do you get it?" — the strategic actions (typically 3–5).
O
Obstacles
"What is preventing you from being successful?" — honest naming of risks & gaps.

How V2MOM actually picks the work

It picks strategic direction, not features. The mechanism is the cascade: CEO writes one, every leader writes one aligned to it, every team writes one aligned to their leader's, every individual writes one aligned to their team's. Misalignment is visible in writing.

1

CEO writes the company V2MOM first

Single page. Vision is one sentence. Methods are the 3–5 strategies — these are what the rest of the company must align to.

2

Each org/team writes their own V2MOM that ladders up

The team's Vision is a slice of the CEO's; the team's Methods support one of the CEO's Methods. If a team's V2MOM doesn't connect, that's a misalignment to debate.

3

Individuals write personal V2MOMs aligned to their team

Everyone, on a regular cadence (Salesforce uses annual; many teams refresh more often). The cumulative effect: each person can point at how their work ladders to the CEO's Vision.

4

Measures get tracked. Methods get revised.

Periodically — Benioff frames it as "constant iteration" — you check Measures and re-write the V2MOM. It is not static; it adapts as reality moves.

Worked example — Benioff's actual 1999 V2MOM

Reconstructed from Behind the Cloud and the Salesforce blog. The point isn't this exact wording — it's that the whole strategy of a company that became a $200B business fits on one page.

Salesforce, 1999 — the founding V2MOM (paraphrased from Benioff)

Vision
Rapidly create a world-class internet company / web site for sales force automation.
Values
World-class organization · Time to market · Functionality · Usability · Value to customer.
Methods
Hire the team · Finalize product specifications & technology · Build prototype · Run beta tests · Launch site.
Obstacles
Hiring engineering talent · Funding · Building rapidly with quality.
Measures
A working web site · Customers using it · Press coverage at launch.

Note the Measures: they are outcomes (working site, customers using it), not output counts (features shipped). And there is no per-feature impact estimate anywhere — that's the point.

What V2MOM does not do

It is not a prioritisation framework. A V2MOM tells your team which direction they're pushing in. It does not tell them whether to ship Feature A or Feature B next quarter. Inside a single "Method," you still need a scoring framework (RICE), an input model (North Star), or experimentation (ExP) to pick the bets that move the Measures.

It is not OKRs. OKRs are an objective + measurable key results, per cycle. V2MOM is a full strategic statement (vision, values, obstacles too) — many companies use V2MOM at leadership level and OKRs at team level. (Asana's Pyramid of Clarity takes the OKR side of the same shape.)

Apply to a feature sheet

V2MOM doesn't score features — it filters them against the team's Methods. If you adopt it, every backlog row must declare which Method it supports and which Measure it moves. Features that don't trace to a Method aren't ranked low; they're out of scope this cycle, full stop.

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Open a blank page. Write your team's V2MOM right now — five lines, ugly first draft. Vision: one sentence. Values: three to five words. Methods: three to five strategic actions. Obstacles: name the real ones. Measures: how you'll know it worked. Then take your team's last sprint backlog and put a "M#" column next to every item. Rows with no Method are the conversation you needed to have.

For the worked example below, assume this team's V2MOM:
Vision: "Be the most-trusted analytics dashboard for product teams."   Methods: M1 — Ship 5 high-quality integrations · M2 — Cut time-to-first-insight to < 2 min · M3 — Reach enterprise-grade SSO & SCIM (SSO = single sign-on; SCIM = automated user provisioning, the two enterprise procurement gates) · M4 — NPS ≥ 50 in mid-market (NPS = Net Promoter Score, a 0–10 "would you recommend" survey)

The most important reading skill on this page. Notice that V2MOM doesn't ask "is this feature good?" — it asks "does this feature trace upward to something we already wrote down?" That's the entire trick. The framework converts the question "what should we build?" into the easier question "what did we already agree to do?" — and makes orphan features visible as a verdict, not an opinion. The four Re-shape/Out verdicts in the table are not value judgments. They are traceability failures, and they're objective.
Decision rule. Three gates. (1) Does the feature name a Method it supports? Orphans are out — that's the V2MOM doing its job. (2) Does it move a Measure that's literally in the V2MOM's Measures section? "Adjacent" doesn't count; re-shape until the Measure is exact. (3) Does it compromise a Value? If yes, it must declare the mitigation. Features passing all three enter the ranked build queue, where you score them with RICE or run experiments. V2MOM doesn't score features — it tells you which ones earn the right to be scored.
Why V2MOM is on the list anyway. Every other framework on this site assumes you've already agreed what you're trying to do. V2MOM is the artifact that produces that agreement and forces it into writing. Without it, RICE scores a wishlist; with it, RICE scores candidates that all support the same Method.
