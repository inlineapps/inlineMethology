Microsoft ExP — OEC, CUPED, and trustworthy experiments at scale 2013

Pioneered by Ron Kohavi and team across Bing, Office, Windows, and Azure. Three pieces — a platform that runs experiments cheaply, a single agreed success metric per test (OEC), and a variance-reduction technique (CUPED) that gets the same answer from far fewer users.

Earliest canonical publication: the CUPED paper (Deng, Xu, Kohavi, Walker — 2013). Codified at book length in Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments (Kohavi, Tang, Xu — 2020).

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
Everyone has opinions about whether a feature will work. Microsoft figured out that opinions are mostly wrong — when you actually test ideas on real users by showing the new thing to half of them and the old thing to the other half (an A/B test), only about one in three ideas actually makes the number you cared about go up. One in three changes nothing. One in three makes it worse. So they built a platform (ExP) that makes running these tests cheap, agreed on a single success number per test (OEC) so nobody can cheat by switching what counts as winning, and invented a math trick (CUPED) that gets the same answer from half as many users — so you find out faster. The whole point: stop arguing about whether ideas will work, just test them.

The honest headline: this is the only ground-truth method on the list

Most frameworks estimate impact (RICE) or model it (North Star). Microsoft's experimentation platform measures it — a controlled A/B test on real users is the only number that's actually causal. The other methods narrow the candidates; this one decides the winner.

The cost of that rigour: it requires traffic, infra, instrumentation, and a culture that accepts only ~1 in 3 ideas actually win. The widely-cited Kohavi finding (paraphrased from his talks and the Kohavi/Tang/Xu book): "When evaluating well-designed and executed experiments that were designed to improve a key metric at Microsoft, only about one-third were successful at improving the key metric." Trustworthy experiments are how you find out which third.

The three pieces — ExP, OEC, CUPED

It's tempting to talk about "doing A/B tests" as if it's one thing. Microsoft separates three distinct problems and gives each a name.

ExP

The platform

Internal experimentation system that lets any team design, randomize, deploy, and analyse experiments without re-building plumbing each time. The platform itself is the cultural enabler — without it, experiments are too expensive to run by default.

OEC

Overall Evaluation Criterion

The one agreed success metric per experiment, decided before the test runs. Without an OEC the team will cherry-pick whichever metric moved. The OEC also embeds long-term thinking (e.g. revenue per session weighted by churn risk) so a team can't win by gaming a short-term proxy.

CUPED

Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data

Variance-reduction technique from Deng, Xu, Kohavi, Walker (WSDM 2013). Use each user's pre-experiment behaviour as a covariate to subtract baseline noise. The Bing slowdown experiment in the paper is the canonical illustration — with CUPED, the +250ms server delay's CTR effect was statistically significant from day 1, whereas the un-adjusted analysis took two weeks at a smaller exposure to reach borderline significance. Three Bing experiments cited in the paper showed variance reductions of 45%, 52%, and 49% — "the same statistical power with about half the users or half the duration." (Variance reduction is metric-dependent: revenue-per-user gained <5%; the DoorDash Dash-AB post reports 10–20% sample reduction.)

Source: exp-platform.com (Kohavi's hub) · CUPED paper (Deng et al., 2013) · book: Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments (Kohavi, Tang, Xu, 2020).

How an ExP-style experiment actually picks the winner

Five steps. The discipline that distinguishes "trustworthy" experiments from "we shipped an A/B test" is that steps 1–2 happen before any code is written.

1

Write the hypothesis and pick the OEC

A single sentence: "If we ship X, we expect the OEC to move by Y, because Z." The OEC is locked before the test starts — no peeking and re-picking.

2

Define guardrail metrics

Metrics that must not get worse: latency, error rate, support volume. Catches the "OEC went up but churn went up too" failure mode.

3

Power-calculate the sample size

How many users for what effect size? CUPED comes in here — by subtracting pre-experiment variance you need ~half as many users for the same confidence.

4

Run the test, then check it ran clean

Sample-Ratio-Mismatch checks, A/A pre-tests, segment analysis. The "trust this readout before reading it" discipline is the central argument of Kohavi / Tang / Xu's Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments book — the specific "~70% of surprising results turn out to be bugs" figure is from Kohavi's talks rather than a published paper, and is repeated here as a cultural finding rather than a hard statistic.

5

Read the OEC. Ship, kill, or iterate.

If the OEC moved and guardrails held, ship. If not, kill — even if you loved it. The book calls this "humility through data."

Worked example — Bing's $100M headline-color test

Tiny change, enormous OEC movement

The most cited Microsoft experiment: a Bing engineer proposed changing the colour and font of result-page headlines. It sat unprioritised for more than six months — until someone ran the A/B test.

"The change had increased revenue by an astonishing 12% — which on an annual basis would come to more than $100 million in the United States alone." — Kohavi & Thomke, HBR (Sept–Oct 2017, verbatim)

The lesson is not "small changes can be big" — it's that without the experiment, no scoring framework would have ranked this above the work it was beating. The team's pre-test estimate was effectively zero. Only measurement found it.

Source: HBR — The Surprising Power of Online Experiments (Kohavi & Thomke).

Caveat: the ⅓ / ⅓ / ⅓ split is the Kohavi-canonical figure (talks, HBR, and the Kohavi/Tang/Xu 2020 book) — a Microsoft-internal aggregate, not a controlled study. Bing's success rate is reportedly lower than the Microsoft-wide ⅓. Other companies (Booking, Netflix) independently cite similar ratios. The $100M Bing figure is HBR-published (reputable secondary).
Why this matters for prioritisation. If only ~1/3 of ideas win, ranking 50 ideas with RICE and shipping the top 10 means you ship ~7 losers and ~3 winners — and you may not know which were which. ExP-style discipline says: ship behind a test, declare the OEC up front, kill what doesn't move it. That's the entire argument for trustworthy experimentation as the layer that finishes what scoring frameworks start.

Apply to a feature sheet

ExP-style discipline doesn't score features pre-build — it adjudicates them post-build via experiment. If you adopt it, your feature sheet evolves from "ideas + scores" into a live experiment ledger: every shipped feature carries the test that earned it the right to stay.

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Pick the most recently-shipped feature on your team. Write its post-hoc experiment design: what was the OEC (the one number that defined winning)? What guardrails should not have regressed? What MDE would have justified the build? If you can't answer those for a feature you already shipped, you've found the discipline gap — your team is shipping ideas faster than you're learning from them. Next sprint: pick one upcoming feature and write those three lines before any code goes in.
Quick glossary for the columns below. OEC = Overall Evaluation Criterion (the one agreed success metric). MDE = Minimum Detectable Effect (the smallest change the test is powered to spot). SRM = Sample-Ratio Mismatch (when your 50/50 split came out 51/49 by accident — a sign the experiment is broken). A/A test = both arms get the same variant; if you see "winning" results, the platform is lying to you. CI = Confidence Interval. CTA = Call-To-Action (the button you want clicked). a11y = accessibility.

The most important reading skill on this page. Look at the "Aggressive paywall variant" row. OEC moved (+8%, a huge win on most teams' default metric). Guardrails breached (support tickets +40%). Decision: Kill. This is the row that explains why ExP exists. A team without guardrails ships the +8% feature and discovers the support cost months later, by which point the feature is entrenched. A team with guardrails sees both numbers in the same readout and kills it the same day. The OEC tells you what you wanted; the guardrails tell you what you forgot you needed.
Decision rule. Ship only if (1) OEC moves with statistical significance, (2) every guardrail holds, and (3) no SRM or trust flag was raised. Iterate if OEC is flat but guardrails are clean — change the variant, re-run. Kill immediately when a guardrail breaches, even if the OEC looks great (the paywall row): a feature that lifts conversion but burns support load is a net loss. The point of CUPED is that you can read these verdicts in days, not weeks — speed of decision is what makes the ⅓-win rate survivable.
