Anthropic side quests — prototype first, let engagement decide

Anthropic's published product approach is, deliberately, not a ranking method. There is no score, no PRD, no quarterly stack-rank. Features are built as "side quests" — afternoon prototypes shipped inside the company; the ones that get used get promoted. The judgement is downstream, not upstream.

Goal
Decide what to ship by seeing what works in real use, not by scoring what might.

The honest headline: this is an anti-method, on purpose

Most pages on this site describe a framework with explicit columns or formulas. The Anthropic one doesn't, and the head of product for Claude Code says so directly. The mechanism is: prototype on company time, ship it inside the building, watch what people use, and promote that. Engagement is the score; the prototype is the spec.

What makes this work — and what makes it hard to copy — is that the prototype-to-internal-ship loop has been compressed to hours, not weeks. The cost of a wrong bet is "an afternoon," so the team can afford to make the bet without ranking it first. That cost structure is the load-bearing assumption.

The loop — five stages, run continuously

No roadmap, no quarterly planning. A side quest enters the loop, runs once, and exits with one of three verdicts: promote, iterate, or drop. Many run in parallel; the loop never stops.

1 · Pick

Idea, sized to an afternoon

Self-directed; off the official roadmap. If it can't be prototyped in a day, re-shape it.

→

2 · Build

Prototype with the model itself

Claude Code writes most of the code. The PM does the prototyping. Capability first, costs later.

→

3 · Ship internal

Dogfood to the company

Posted in an internal launch channel — no review gate, no PRD. Anyone at Anthropic can try it.

→

4 · Watch

Engagement is the signal

Who actually uses it. Qualitative reactions. Usage telemetry. No pre-declared OEC — the signal is "are people coming back?"

→

5 · Decide

Promote, iterate, or drop

Real engagement → polish & ship externally. Mixed → keep prototyping. None → drop, no post-mortem.

Loop is continuous — the next side quest is already in stage 1 while this one is in stage 4.
"A side quest is a short self-directed experiment you run outside your official roadmap… The ones with real engagement get polished and shared more broadly. Because you can prototype in an afternoon, wrong bets are cheap."— Cat Wu, Head of Product for Claude Code, "Product management on the AI exponential" (Mar 19, 2026)

What this approach explicitly throws out

The Anthropic post is unusual in being directly anti-something. Worth listing what's missing so the reader can decide if those things mattered.

The traditional PM stack — discarded

  • PRDs / written specs ahead of build
  • Quarterly roadmap stack-rank
  • Scoring formulas (RICE, ICE, weighted)
  • Pre-build impact estimation
  • Kill criteria written before the test
  • Cost-per-token optimisation early in build

What it kept (or built in their place)

  • Prototype-first ("demos over docs")
  • Side-quest queue, refreshed continuously
  • Engagement signal from internal dogfood
  • "Capability first" — over-spend tokens, optimise later
  • "Do the simple thing that works" — newer models will close the gap
  • Re-test old prototypes when a new model drops

Decision rule. Promote when the engagement signal is unambiguously "real" — people seek it out, come back, and the team would notice if it were removed. Iterate when usage is mixed; re-prototype, don't write a PRD. Drop with no post-mortem when no one uses it; the prototype already paid for itself by answering the question. Park when the capability ceiling is the bottleneck — re-run the loop the day the next model ships. The whole rule is "the model is the moving target; build for it, not around it."

Where Anthropic's approach breaks (be honest about this)

It needs an internal dogfood pool of the right shape. The engagement signal only works because Anthropic's staff are the target user (technical, AI-native, willing to try half-built things). A consumer team with no proxy user inside the building gets zero signal from internal ship.

It abdicates upstream prioritisation. The method works for AI-product features Anthropic is going to discover one way or the other. It doesn't help when there's a strategic choice between unrelated bets — that decision is made elsewhere (the post explicitly says "clear strategy and goals let everyone prioritize autonomously"), and the side-quest loop doesn't address how that strategy is set.

It is structurally biased toward the discoverable. A side quest is, by definition, prototype-able in an afternoon. Multi-quarter infrastructure work, regulatory work, and anything contract-shaped doesn't fit the loop. Anthropic's RSP (separate document) is what handles the high-cost-of-wrong items.

The "anti-method" framing is overstated outside Claude Code. Cat Wu's post is specifically about how her team works on a developer-tools product. The public PM plugin (anthropics/knowledge-work-plugins) ships with RICE, MoSCoW, JTBD and HMW as supported tools — so it's clear that Anthropic's own opinion is "use a framework if it helps; we don't need one for this product."
Why it's on the list. Two reasons. First, the user asked specifically whether AI labs have published a feature-decision method — and this is the one direct on-the-record publication from either lab. Second, the "cost of a wrong bet is an afternoon" cost-structure claim is the load-bearing assumption every traditional method makes opposite; seeing the inversion stated plainly is useful for our own decision about how much pre-build estimation to do.
