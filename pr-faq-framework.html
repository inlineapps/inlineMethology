Amazon Working Backwards / PR-FAQ — write the launch before the code 2004

A mock press release plus an FAQ — both written before a line of code is shipped. Used at Amazon since 2004 across both consumer (Kindle, Prime, Echo) and B2B (AWS) products.

The discipline: if you can't write a compelling PR for the customer, you don't yet have a feature worth building.

Goal
Decide features by data-backed expected impact — choose by outcome, not by to-do list or opinion.
🎓 8th-grade version
Before Amazon builds anything, somebody writes the news article announcing it — like it's already done and being sold. One page. Then a list of all the boring questions: how much does it cost, who's it for, what could go wrong, how do we know if it worked? If the news article sounds dumb or fake when people read it, the idea isn't ready — you change the idea, not the writing. Most ideas die at this step, before any code is written. The ones that survive get built, because by then they're worth building.

The honest headline: the strongest pre-build forcing function on the list

Most "estimation" methods score features after the team has decided what to build. PR-FAQ inverts the order: you write the customer-facing launch first. If the press release reads as boring, irrelevant, or implausible, you re-shape the problem — long before you've sunk engineering effort. Ex-Amazon execs Colin Bryar & Bill Carr describe it (verbatim) as "a systematic way to vet ideas and create new products. Its key tenet is to start by defining the customer experience, then iteratively work backwards from that point until the team achieves clarity of thought around what to build."

It's the only method on this site that produces a readable artifact a non-PM can react to. That's why it travels well — but also why it's hard: writing one good PR-FAQ takes days, not minutes.

The artifact — what a PR-FAQ actually contains

Two parts, both short. The strict format is the discipline: limited length forces clarity.

PR — Press release (≤1 page)

Reads exactly like a real announcement

  • Headline — the product, in the language the customer would use.
  • Subheading — who it's for and the key benefit.
  • Summary — one paragraph the customer would understand.
  • Problem — the specific pain being solved (in customer's words).
  • Solution — how the product solves it.
  • Quote from a leader — captures the strategic intent.
  • How to get started — what the customer does next.
  • Customer quote — a hypothetical real reaction.
FAQ — Internal & external (≤5 pages)

The honest part — where edge cases get surfaced

  • External FAQ — questions a customer or journalist would ask: price, how it works, support, where to buy.
  • Internal FAQ — questions senior leaders and stakeholders would ask. The source lists: competitors, problems solved, TAM (Total Addressable Market), challenges, capabilities needed, dependencies, regulatory issues, unit economics, investment required, payback timeline, success assumptions, failure risks.
  • Success metrics live in the Internal FAQ — explicit numeric outcomes ("we expect X% adoption in 6 months because…"). This is the line that makes PR-FAQ a pre-build impact estimator.
Source: About Amazon — culture & processes · Working Backwards (Bryar & Carr) — PR-FAQ process.

How a PR-FAQ actually picks the work

The mechanism is iterative and brutal: most first drafts get killed. Bezos's well-known rule: the PR-FAQ must be revised until the room can't poke a useful hole in it.

1

Pick the customer and the bad-day moment

Start from a real customer in a specific situation. The PR will be written for them, not for the org chart.

2

Draft the PR

Write the launch as if it ships tomorrow. If the headline is weak, the product is weak. The team often discovers in this step that the feature they wanted to build isn't compelling to the customer.

3

Write the FAQ — both halves

External FAQ surfaces customer objections; Internal FAQ surfaces team disagreements and forces numeric success metrics. This is where "expected impact" becomes a written number.

4

Review in a silent-read meeting

Amazon's distinctive ritual (verbatim from the source): "The document is circulated at the start of the meeting, followed by 15–20 minutes of silent reading and note-taking." No slides, no selling — the doc has to stand on its own. Most bets die here.

5

Iterate or kill

If the doc survives critique, work begins from the PR backwards — what would have to be built to make the PR true? Many candidates iterate the PR for weeks before any code is written.

Worked example — the original Kindle PR-FAQ (the famous story)

1-page PR, written before any Kindle hardware existed

Headline: "Introducing Kindle — every book ever printed, in any language, all available in under 60 seconds."

Subheading: Read books anywhere, instantly, without losing your library.

Key Internal-FAQ pressure tests it had to answer: Why would a reader pay for a device? How does it work without Wi-Fi (→ became free 3G)? What if publishers won't sign? What does success look like in year 1?

The Kindle PR-FAQ went through many iterations before the device was built. The team didn't just design the Kindle from the PR — they kept revising the PR until the customer story was undeniable.

Source for the Kindle PR-FAQ narrative: Bryar & Carr, Working Backwards, Chapter 5. The "every book ever printed... in under 60 seconds" line is Bezos's well-known Kindle vision — used here as a stand-in for the headline. The actual PR-FAQ text was never published by Amazon; the structure and discipline are the verifiable parts. AWS is a more documented example: Bryar & Carr describe the AWS team spending "more than a year writing, revising, debating, and discussing PR/FAQs" between identifying web services in 2004 and launching S3/EC2 in 2006.

Apply to a feature sheet

PR-FAQ doesn't produce a numeric score either — it produces an artifact gate. Your sheet doesn't rank features; it tracks readiness. A feature does not enter build until every cell is green and the silent-read review passes. The columns are a checklist, not a calculation.

Try it Monday morning (30 minutes). Pick your next "big bet" feature. Open a doc. Write only the headline and the subheading of its press release — as if it shipped tomorrow. Don't write the body. Just those two lines. Show them to one person who doesn't know the feature. If they can't tell you who it's for and why they'd care, the bet isn't shaped yet. That's a 30-minute version of the PR-FAQ pain — small enough to do this week, painful enough to surface the truth.

Decision rule. Ready for build only when every column is green and the silent-read room can't poke a useful hole in the doc. Re-draft if any field is missing or weak — go back to step 2, no engineering effort yet. Kill when the headline can't be made compelling after iteration; that's the PR-FAQ doing its job — most first drafts die at this gate, which is the point. PR-FAQ filters at the bet level; once a bet is built, individual changes still go through experiment-style A/B readouts.

Where PR-FAQ breaks

It's expensive. Days to weeks per artifact. Reserve PR-FAQs for big bets, not every small change. For routine work use RICE-style ranking.

It can ossify the product. If the PR was wrong, the team is anchored to it. Mitigation: explicit "kill criteria" in the Internal FAQ — what would convince us to stop?

It is not a substitute for experimentation. The PR-FAQ estimates expected impact; controlled experiments measure actual. Amazon does both — PR-FAQ at the bet level, A/B at the change level.
Why PR-FAQ is on the list. It's the only Consumer-side framework that produces an up-front written estimate of customer value rather than relying on post-launch measurement. The closest cousin in the SaaS set is Stripe's deep-dive memo, but PR-FAQ adds the strict format that makes it teachable.
